## Statement

> For an engaged citizen who cares about conservation and wants to learn more about birds and wants all the data and info in one app
> who wants to use the data points of each species of bird in a location to take action and advocate for the birds in the habitat and spread awareness,
> the Birdpoint is an interactive data tool
> that gives users accessible bird population data to identify at-risk species and take informed conservation action.
> Unlike Google and scattered online sources,
> our product allows users to see trends of the bird data points all in one place.

## Evidence

One subsection per clause. Updated continuously across all five passes.

### Target customer

- **Current belief:** An engaged citizen who cares about conservation and wants to learn more about birds and wants all the data and info in one app.
- **Basis for the belief:** Origin interview with friend (birdwatcher/conservationist). She described frustration with switching between multiple apps and databases. She's not a researcher but actively digs into population data to understand ecological change. Student's instinct that habitat protectors ā€” not casual hikers or researchers ā€” would care most about multi-year trend data.
- **Evidence found:**
  - 2026-06-26, origin interview: Friend described bridging eBird, BirdonMap, GIS tools, and academic portals to answer one ecological question (post-wildfire species recovery). Said "there isn't a unified space that connects casual field logging with deep, meaningful data analysis."
- **Alternatives considered:** Casual hiker (too low motivation to use deep data); serious researcher (already has professional tools); general learner (too broad, doesn't explain why multi-year data matters to them)
- **What would change my mind:** If interviews show that people who care about conservation already have tools they're happy with, or that the real pain point belongs to a different user type.

### Need or opportunity

- **Current belief:** Users want to use data points of each bird species in a location to take action, advocate for habitat protection, and spread awareness.
- **Basis for the belief:** Origin interview ā€” friend described going into population databases after a wildfire to understand species recovery. She wasn't just curious; she wanted to understand ecological impact. Student's inference that understanding precedes action.
- **Evidence found:**
  - 2026-06-26, origin interview: Friend said she dug into spatial distribution data over a multi-year timeline to see if species were abandoning burn scars. She treated an out-of-range sighting as a "mini-research project" to understand environmental causes.
- **Alternatives considered:** "Who wants to identify birds in the field" ā€” dropped because the interview pointed more strongly to trend/context than field ID as the primary need.
- **What would change my mind:** If interviews show users mainly want field ID or casual logging, not trend analysis or advocacy.

### Product category

- **Current belief:** Interactive data tool
- **Basis for the belief:** Student chose it as more precise than "educational app" ā€” reflects the hands-on, data-driven nature of the product.
- **Evidence found:** (none yet)
- **Alternatives considered:** Educational app (too passive), conservation app (implies action over learning), platform (too broad)
- **What would change my mind:** If users describe themselves as looking for an "app" or "map" rather than a "tool" ā€” category expectations shape what they expect to find inside.

### Key benefit

- **Current belief:** Gives users accessible bird population data to identify at-risk species and take informed conservation action.
- **Basis for the belief:** Origin interview ā€” friend had to bridge multiple tools to get population trend data. The app removes that friction and puts the data in front of the user directly.
- **Evidence found:**
  - 2026-06-26, origin interview: Friend described needing to "bridge the gap between casual birding apps and actual academic or university research databases" to get real population numbers.
- **Alternatives considered:** "Knowing they made a positive difference" ā€” dropped because it's downstream of the app; "data and information" ā€” too feature-focused, not a benefit.
- **What would change my mind:** If users say they can already get this data easily elsewhere, or that they don't actually use population data to make decisions.

### Primary competitive alternative

- **Current belief:** Google and scattered online sources.
- **Basis for the belief:** Student's inference that most non-expert users start with Google. Origin interview confirmed the fragmentation problem ā€” friend bridges eBird, BirdonMap, GIS tools, academic portals.
- **Evidence found:**
  - 2026-06-26, origin interview: Friend described the fragmentation: "If I want to identify a call on the fly, I use one app. If I want to log a sighting, I open another. If I want to understand the actual ecological context or population trends of that area, I have to go home, open my laptop, and dig through academic portals or GIS mapping tools."
- **Alternatives considered:** Rangers/experts (access is limited and inconsistent); specialized apps like eBird (serve birdwatchers but not conservation-focused citizens who want trend data).
- **What would change my mind:** If interviews show users are already using a specific tool they're happy with, or that rangers are the primary resource for most people.

### Primary differentiation

- **Current belief:** Allows users to see trends of bird data points all in one place.
- **Basis for the belief:** The NEON dataset covers 2017ā€“2022 across multiple U.S. ecosystems ā€” multi-year, multi-location data that isn't consolidated anywhere accessible to non-researchers. Friend had to assemble this herself from multiple sources.
- **Evidence found:**
  - 2026-06-26, origin interview: Friend described wanting "the actual spatial distribution data over a multi-year timeline" and having to build that view herself from separate tools.
- **Alternatives considered:** "Simpler interface than academic tools" ā€” true but not the core differentiator; "built-in ID features" ā€” dropped, field ID is not the primary use case.
- **What would change my mind:** If a competing app already does this well and users know about it.
